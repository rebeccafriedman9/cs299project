Project Overview & Motivations

The Backfire Effect

The backfire effect, in which a correction actually works to increase belief in the misinformation it is attempting to disprove, is a far less common phenomenon than once believed. One should not avoid correcting misinformation in fear that doing so will create stronger belief in such misinformation.(Wood & Porter (2019); Swire‑Thompson et al. (2020)).

One of the most concerning notions for science communicators, fact-checkers, and advocates of truth, is the backfire effect: when a correction leads an individual to increase belief in the very misconception the correction is aiming to rectify.

Swire‑Thompson et al. (2020)
Failure to Replicate

Recent empirical studies have consistently failed to replicate the backfire effect, even when conditions were favorable to do so. In regards to the worldview backfire effect, this involves the correction targeting a piece of misinformation central to one’s worldview, and within the familiarity backfire effect, this would involve repeated exposure to misinformation. (Swire‑Thompson et al. (2020)). Early studies, such as Nyhan & Reifler (2015) on attempts to correct misinformation about the flu vaccine demonstrated strong backfire effects. Yet Kathryn Haglin’s replication of this study did not reproduce the backfire effect; Haglin (2017). Similarly, Wood & Porter (2019) present results from five experiments that tested 52 issues on over 10,000 subjects: “Across all experiments, we found no corrections capable of triggering backfire….”

Swire‑Thompson et al. (2020), in an extensive literature review, go so far as to suggest that “backfire effects are not a robust empirical phenomenon,” emphasizing that “improved measures, more powerful designs, and stronger links between experimental design and theory could greatly help move the field ahead.”


Implications for Debunkers

One should not avoid correcting misinformation in fear that doing so will reinforce belief in the misinformation. The worldview and familiarity backfire have been shown to occur far less frequently than once believed.

StudyBuddy: Ethics and Impact

To analyze a debunker in the wild, I looked at the video published by Insider Science in 2021 titled, "Podiatrists Debunk 11 Feet Myths". This video is a part of their Debunked series, in which they have professionals debunk common myths in their domain of expertise. I specifically looked at myth #8, "it's normal for your feet to hurt from standing all day" to analyze the strategies, both within and beyond the debunking handbook, used to debunk this specific myth.

Handbook Methodologies

Structuring a Debunking Argument

Handbook Debunking Structure: Fact → Myth → Fallacy → Fact

Debunking Structure Used: Myth → Fact → Fallacy → Fact

The video segment’s debunking structure slightly deviates from the handbook model by starting with the myth first rather than the fact. The myth is presented in two modes, visually on a printed sign and through a verbal statement. The video also violates the handbook’s principle that misinformation should not be repeated, as the sign displaying the myth is present throughout the entire segment. These are not effective persuasive techniques for debunking misinformation, as they perpetuate the illusory truth effect, in which repeated information is perceived as true simply because it is more familiar.

The second half of the debunking adheres more closely to the handbook guidelines. The approach identifies and explains the fallacy behind the misinformation, and then moves beyond a simple retraction of the myth by explaining why the misinformation is incorrect. The segment is ultimately ended by reinforcing the fact that proper foot health is important, and it is not normal for one’s feet to hurt even after standing all day.

Persuasive Techniques from the Handbook

Establishing credibility

The handbook also highlights that for communication to be effective, the communicators and source must be perceived as credible by the audience. This video establishes credibility in multiple ways. Even before establishing credibility in the introduction to the video as the podiatrists explain their backgrounds, expertise is acknowledged in the title and description of the video. Each video in the Debunked series follows the title structure of [experts] debunk [number] myths in [their domain]. The description of the video provides more background information on each doctor and links to where you can find out more about their work. In order for information to be persuasive, it must first be perceived as credible, thus establishing expertise in the field is imperative to the success of the debunking.

Avoiding jargon

The video follows the handbook guideline of avoiding unnecessary, complex jargon so that the information is accessible to a broad audience.

Diagrams

Diagrams are utilized to present complex information in a more easily understandable format.

Persuasive Techniques Beyond the Handbook

Utilizing the affordances of YouTube

The text overlays highlight key information, while the video clips demonstrate relevant content in an engaging way. Most of the video is simply the two podiatrists sitting down and talking to the camera in this clean, professional environment. Thus, when short clips demonstrating relevant content are embedded, this grabs and holds the attention of the viewer by introducing visual variety.

Building the Gem

I created a social media infographic debunking the myth that constraints limit creativity. To ground the claims and design decisions I referenced a small set of studies focused on definitions, structure, and empirical tests of constraints on creativity.

Specifically, Dimock (1986) helped frame my definition of creativity, while empirical studies by Sagiv et al. (2010) and Kern (2006) supported the arguement. Moreau & Dahl (2005) and Stokes (2006) also provided empirical evidence and framing examples showing how constraints can augment creativity across different domains.
